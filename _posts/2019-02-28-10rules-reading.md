## Remarks

- Reading is as important as writing. It give us context and depth of our field. 
- Make it a habit (at breakfast, at coffee, etc). One paper/day.
- Organize the papers in __read__ and __to read__ (but be honest about this) with the help of a ref manager (e.g., Mendeley). Don't let literature accumulate chaotically.
- Keep track of what you read (spreadsheet or text). This will be useful when writing a paper (research or review).

## The rules

### Rule 1. Read on a daily basis

Don't just pile papers. The author says this create knowledge delusion.

Urgent reading just to fill a paper in preparation is not efficient nor desirable. 

At least one paper a day. Find the time: breakfast, with a coffee before starting to work, while in public transport... What is important is to make it an habit.

Personal advice: make use of the tools from the field of Systematic reviews, like the data bases of Scopus and Web of Science, to search for literature related with your field. Those tools are worth the time to learn. 

### Rule 2. Read thoroughly

To build a sound background. This will provide broad context about: 

> concepts, methods, results, and potential meanings and implications 

The best time to do this is in the graduate period. PIs doesn't have the time while teaching, supervising and managing projects. So, yes, the PhD period is the time to read *a lot*.

A second advantage: the feedback of reading is reading better and selectively. Once you are familiar with the concepts and methodology, the process is faster and the search of sources narrows.

### Rule 3. Read the classics

So you don't reinvent the wheel. Read the foundational papers of the discipline. Science is self-improving, but this doesn't mean our jargon and theory are recent inventions. The classics pointed the directions, concepts and general framework and context, even if we have new methods and theories. The concept of reinforcement might not be the same that Skinner used, as well as the concept of habit in Hull, but can we distinguish where are the differences, and how we got into this point?

### Rule 4. In getting familiar with a new topic, read it in chronological order

This will allow you to get a hint of the status and, most important, to see the directions, the speed of change or the lethargic state of the field. All the research have be done in recent years or in a longer lapse of time? The field is in need of a different point of view? 

### Rule 5. Read beyond your discipline

Verbatim:

> Interesting ideas, concepts, methods, or implications are waiting... Find a balance between focused and broad reading.

One way to achieve this going to journal clubs or to use social networks to find recommendations.

Behavior Analysis is not telling the whole history of behavior. Topics of interest: economics, cognitive science, neuroscience, machine learning and artificial intelligence. 

### Rule 6. Create a list of relevant journals

JEAB is not telling the whole history of Behavior Analysis. Journals of interest can be found in APA, the Association for Psychological Science (APS), Psychonomic Society and Open Source initiatives like PsyArxiv or Open Science Framework.

Don't forget the journals of analytical, experimental or statistical methods. 

### Rule 7. Read books

> Books destil accumulated knowledge or suggest groundbreaking ideas. 

Possibilities: mailing lists or alerts (in Google scholar) about specific topics, journal sections for reviews of new books or the old reliable: go to the library of your university (or to the office of your PI). 

### Rule 8. Use reference manager

To avoid chaotic accumulation (like myself). Rules about this rule: 

- update recent readings, 
- never store papers you haven't read, or
- make a _to read_ folder (aha!) subdivided in topics of urgency, 
- create personalized list of keywords (it helps to build a better background).

### Rule 9. Keep a long-term review for your own use as a way to remember what you read _(like this one)_

> The more you read, the more you forget

Having a spreadsheet or a text document to store basic info or main messages from the primary literature is a good practice. Allow us to retrieve easily the information we vaguely remember (useful when writing an intro or discussion in a paper, or a review). 

### Rule 10. Build your own library

For independence and inspiration. Important for mentoring. 

> for those of us who grew intellectually surrounded by books and journals in our departmental library the mere presence of books around creates an inspiring atmosphere

I support this idea 100 %, but I think it's because I love books. However, I can add a second advantage: reading in a computer can be _very, very_ distracting (too much stimuli or temptations), so, I have found more productive to have printed books and papers to isolate myself in a better atmosphere to read and think. 

> science is about the excitement of discovery and the amusement
> of enhanced understanding. Reading contributes to both.

Hell yeah!
